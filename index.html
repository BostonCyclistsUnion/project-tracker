Glossary

Many of the terms used in our project details may be unfamiliar or you may wonder why we track some information. We have elaborated on these details below.

Project Stage

Proposed

The project has been discussed among city planners but isn’t officially happening yet.

Announced

The city or municipality has agreed to explore the project.

Pre-25% design

The project team has started design work but hasn’t presented anything to the public yet.

25% design

Concepts of plans are outlined for discussion and evaluation.

75% design

Specifics of elements are outlined and evaluated with engineers and community members.

100% design

Design is complete.

Under construction

The project is being built.

Partially undone

Some construction has happened, and the project may have been considered complete, but parts have been removed.

Complete

Work is done.

Construction Type

Full Construction

Sometimes called Full-Depth Construction. These are major capital projects that reconstruct the full street, often including upgrading underground utilities. In these projects, moving the curbline and modifying drainage are possible, permitting more complete street transformations such as sidewalk-grade bike lanes. These are the longest projects to build, especially when utilities are affected. In the case that utilities are upgraded, each utility will perform their upgrades in sequence. There is often a significant delay from one utility provider finishing to the next one starting their work, extending the overall construction timeline.

Quick-Build

These projects are the fastest to implement, with construction usually completed within months and only one construction season. The primary tool for changing a street design is updating paint markings and signage. Flex-posts, precast curbs, and other low-cost materials that can be installed on top of the roadway may be used to reinforce the markings and increase separation.

Partial Construction

A project that is mostly quick-build, but has an expanded budget to perform some construction at critical areas of the design. Common examples are removing a curb extension to make the bike lane more direct and reduce parking loss, building a floating bus stop where the bike lane travels behind the boarding area of the stop, or removing a median to have more space to fit all desired design elements. The construction level will usually not affect underground utilities, preventing construction delays from coordinating with utility providers.

Responsible Parties

Owner

The agency or municipality that is leading the project.

Designer

The design firm performing the majority of the engineering work.

Constructor

The construction contractor responsible for building the project.

Point(s) Of Contact

The agency staff who are directing the project. They interface with all agency staff, electeds, design firm and construction contractor contacts and ensure the project is meeting the requirements and goals of the project owner.
